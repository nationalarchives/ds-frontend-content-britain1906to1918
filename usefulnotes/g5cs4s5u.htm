Extract from a War Office document on employment of women in the RFC, April 1917
(PRO ref: WO 32/5530)		  
  • This extract is part of a long and detailed document (6 pages) setting out the terms and conditions for women working in the Royal Flying Corps (RFC).
  • The RFC was only formed in the Great War, and was a very new area of warfare. As a result, new production processes were needed, as well as large teams of skilled and semi-skilled workers to keep the aeroplanes in a good state of repair.
  • At first, the RFC resisted women workers, but like most other sectors they recognised women's skills by 1917.
  • The number of working women went up from about 5 million to about 6 million between 1914 and 1918. Perhaps the more striking change was the way in which women took over men's' jobs as the men were called up to fight.
  • This was especially true in engineering and munitions. Around 950,000 women worked in the munitions industries and about 200,000 worked in engineering.
