Extract from a report produced by the Mines Department, November 1916
(PRO ref: POWE 16/176)		  
  • This extract comes from a collection of documents that were found in the Board of Trade in 1925 and sent to the Mines Department. The Board of Trade wanted the Mines Department to either look after them or destroy them!
  • The documents deal with the situation late in 1916 and early in 1917 when the government was preparing to take over the actual running of the coal industry. A major reason for government control was continuing disputes between mine owners and miners. These had been bitter before the war and continued to be bitter after the war, ending in the General Strike of 1926.
  • Coal production was a major problem for the government throughout the war. The key problem was the vast demand for coal. This came from war industries (eg munitions) and related industries (especially iron and steel). Britain also supplied many of her allies with coal. Both Russia and France lost a lot of their coal industries when the Germans invaded their territories.
  • A factor which held back increases in coal production was that so many miners volunteered for the army in 1914. In 1915 the government actually made miners a reserved occupation and prevented them from joining up!
