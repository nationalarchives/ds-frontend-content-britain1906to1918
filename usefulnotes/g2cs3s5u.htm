Letter to Lloyd George from a London Coroner on the death of an old age pensioner
(PRO ref: T172/123)		  
  • Old Age Pensions were introduced in 1908 and were widely welcomed. The most serious criticism of the pension was that it was not enough.
  • Despite the pension, many old people still lived in poverty and in terrible housing. One of these was Edward Heath, who died in April 1913.
  • The Coroner carried out his inquest into Edward Heath's death. The Jury at the inquest decided that Edward Heath had died of heart failure brought on by self-neglect. His body was found in a filthy room, which he shared with his sister in the centre of London. She died soon after from pneumonia.
  • The main concern of the Coroner and the Jury was that the pension was simply given to the old man and his sister without anyone asking whether the pension was enough to live on.
  • When the Jury reached their verdict, the coroner sent his report in. However, the Jury at the inquest wanted to add an extra statement to the Coroner's report. This extra statement was called a rider.
