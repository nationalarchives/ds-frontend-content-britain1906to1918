Extract from a document on the need for Old Age Pensions. It was presented to the Cabinet by the Chancellor of the Exchequer in 1906
(PRO ref: CAB 37/85/96)		  
  • This document was produced in December 1906 and presented to the Cabinet by the Chancellor of the Exchequer Herbert Asquith.
  • In 1906 most politicians realised that something had to be done to relieve poverty among old people. However, they did not agree exactly what had to be done. This was partly because they did not all agree on the causes of poverty for old people. This document tries to examine the organisations which help old people and how effective they are.
  • Friendly Societies were organisations of working people which went back to at least the 1700s. Workers paid a small amount to the society each week. When a member was ill or injured, the worker received sick pay to cover his lost wages.
  • By the early 1900s Friendly Societies allowed workers to save for expensive events like funerals. The largest Friendly Society was the Co-Operative Society.
  • Superannuation schemes were basically pension schemes. People saved throughout their lives so they had money to live on when they retired. Very few working people could afford to save for these.
  • The Chancellor talks about the term 'universal state grant'. This simply means a scheme for old age pensions paid for by the government.
