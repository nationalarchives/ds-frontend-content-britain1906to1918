Cartoon from the Daily Mirror commenting on strikes, 31 August 1918
(W K Haselden, British Cartoon Archive for the Centre for the Study of Cartoons & Caricature at the University of Kent, WH2366)		  
  • This cartoon was published in the Daily Mirror on 31 August 1918. It was one of a series of humorous cartoons that covered tram strikes and power strikes. There were also several silly cartoons about what would happen if the dogs went on strike etc!
  • People are often surprised by the fact that during the Great War there were many strikes. In fact there were millions of working days lost through strikes in the whole of the war, and 1918 was the worst year of all.
  • The key problem was usually bad relations between the employers and the trade unions. Before the war industrial relations had been very bad, with many strikes and lockouts. These often led to violence.
  • This bad feeling continued into the war. It is probably fair to say that unions were excessively suspicious of the employers. However, the past actions of the employers made it easy to see why unions were suspicious of any attempt by the government to control wages or working conditions.
  • The government usually found itself trying to keep the peace with the unions. It had to back down in several major strikes such in the Clyde metalworkers strike (1915), the engineering strike (1917) and a second major engineers strike in April 1918 (when the German army had advanced to within 50 miles of Paris).
  • According to historian Niall Ferguson, only Russia had a worse record of industrial relations than Britain in the Great War.
