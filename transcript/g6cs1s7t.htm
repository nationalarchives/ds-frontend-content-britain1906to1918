WHAT I SAW IN FRANCE

By R. Palmer
(National Union of General Workers)

Munition Dump Worth £12,000,000

Next we saw a great munition dump, and it was calculated that the value of the material in this place was £12,000,000. ……

I could not help thinking of a few years ago when we were asked for £1,000,000 to provide work for the unemployed in order to construct some big roads across England, which the Government would probably have been very glad of now, and we were told that the money could not be found. ……

In a Land of Cemeteries

One has a curious feeling as one motors along the French roads; the trees are almost cut off to their roots as a result of bombardments, and all around are nothing but cemeteries. They are marked by simple crosses with the words "To an Unknown English Soldier," " To an Unknown Canadian Soldier", and so on. Many people at home will never know where their boys lie buried, as sometimes when they have been interred a shell has come over and disturbed the grave again. I would mention the names of the towns that we visited, but I suppose this would not be right. ……

What the Soldiers Said

Other gruesome things are also there which I cannot tell. I met many soldiers, and I took every opportunity of talking with them. Everyone with whom I spoke said, "You at home have got to finish this b------- war, we cannot do it out here." When I said to them, "What about the terms?" they would answer, "B------ the terms. What do we care so long as we get the b------- thing finished? You have got to do it at home."

When I pointed out to them what happened at home in connection with meetings which were described by the Press as peace meetings (whether they were or not), their reply was, "Let those people at home who are killing Huns with their tongues come and have a bit of it, they would not break up meetings then."
